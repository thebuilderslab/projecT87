Additional Factors to Consider
On-Chain Allowance and Approval State

Ensure the contract has allowance for the tokens needed for the swap (e.g., cTokens, aTokens, or the underlying). If allowance is insufficient, some routers silently revert.

Gas Estimation and Limits

While typical limits are used, some swaps need above-average gas due to path complexity. Bumping the gas limit just above the estimation (but not too high) might help, especially in congested periods.

Oracle or Price Feed Issues

Sometimes, if the ParaSwap adapter or Aave relies on oracles, sudden feed changes or stale oracle data may silently cause internal validation failures. Double-check oracle sync or last update times.

Contract Upgradeability or Implementation Bugs

If using a proxy/upgradeable contract architecture (common with Aave/ParaSwap), confirm you’re interacting with the latest implementation. Bugs in old adapters can silently revert without detailed reasons.

Network Congestion and Mempool Frontrunning

Delays in transaction inclusion can cause your otherwise-valid route to become stale (or even fail due to sandwich attacks). Consider adding some mempool monitoring or higher-priority gas settings.

Hidden Transfer Fees or Token Peculiarities

If using tokens with transfer fees, rebase logic, or non-standard ERC-20s, the adapter might revert if transfer amounts are incorrect.

Simulate Before Sending

You already flagged it—simulating the transaction (e.g., via Tenderly, Hardhat, or Alchemy Trace) could reveal the exact opcode and point of failure. This extra diagnostics step is highly encouraged for stubborn cases.

Chain Reorgs or Nonce Issues

Rare, but worth a check: chain reorganizations or nonce conflicts may lead to unexpected reverts, especially during high on-chain activity.
